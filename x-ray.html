Introduction to X-rays

Welcome to the X-ray Imaging Fundamentals course. This module will introduce you to the basic concepts of X-ray imaging, its history, and its importance in modern medicine.

What are X-rays?

X-rays are a form of electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths shorter than visible light. They were discovered in 1895 by Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen, who received the first Nobel Prize in Physics for this discovery.

Modern digital X-ray machine

Basic Principles of X-ray Imaging

X-ray imaging works on the following principles:

  • X-rays are produced when high-energy electrons collide with a metal target
  • Different tissues absorb X-rays to varying degrees (radiodensity)
  • The X-rays that pass through the patient are captured to form an image
  • Dense structures (like bone) appear white, while air appears black

Medical Applications

X-rays are used for various diagnostic purposes:

  1. Chest X-rays: Evaluate lungs, heart, and chest structures
  2. Abdominal X-rays: Assess bowel, kidneys, and other abdominal organs
  3. Musculoskeletal: Detect fractures, dislocations, and bone abnormalities
  4. Dental: Examine teeth and jaw structures
  5. Mammography: Specialized X-ray for breast tissue examination

Safety Considerations

While X-rays are invaluable diagnostic tools, they do involve ionizing radiation. Key safety principles include:

  • ALARA principle (As Low As Reasonably Achievable)
  • Proper shielding with lead aprons and barriers
  • Minimizing repeat exposures
  • Special precautions for pregnant patients

Radiation protection equipment

Key Terminology

Familiarize yourself with these essential terms:

  • Radiolucent: Allows X-rays to pass through (appears dark)
  • Radiopaque: Blocks X-rays (appears white)
  • kVp: Kilovoltage peak - controls X-ray beam penetration
  • mAs: Milliampere-seconds - controls quantity of X-rays
  • Contrast: Difference in density between adjacent structures
