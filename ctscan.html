Master the principles, techniques, and interpretation of CT imaging

Introduction to CT Scanning

Computed Tomography (CT) scanning is a medical imaging technique that uses computer-processed combinations of many X-ray measurements taken from different angles to produce cross-sectional (tomographic) images of specific areas of a scanned object, allowing the user to see inside the object without cutting.

Modern CT Scanner with Advanced Imaging Capabilities

How CT Scans Work

A CT scan works by using a rotating X-ray tube and digital X-ray detectors positioned opposite each other in a circular gantry. As the patient moves through the machine, the X-ray tube rotates around the body, emitting narrow beams of X-rays that pass through the body and are detected on the opposite side.

The computer processes this information to create cross-sectional images or "slices" of the body. These slices can be viewed individually or stacked together to create 3D images of the patient's anatomy.

Key Components of a CT System

  • Gantry: The donut-shaped part that contains the X-ray tube and detectors
  • X-ray Tube: Generates the X-ray beams
  • Detectors: Capture the X-rays that pass through the body
  • Patient Table: Moves the patient through the gantry
  • Computer System: Processes the data to create images

Common CT Scan Protocols

Chest CT 1-2.5mm 120 100-200 Often used

CT Image Interpretation Basics

When interpreting CT images, radiologists evaluate several key aspects:

  1. Anatomy: Identify normal anatomical structures
  2. Density: Assess tissue density (measured in Hounsfield units)
  3. Pathology: Look for abnormalities or disease processes
  4. Artifacts: Recognize and account for imaging artifacts
  5. Clinical Correlation: Relate findings to patient history
